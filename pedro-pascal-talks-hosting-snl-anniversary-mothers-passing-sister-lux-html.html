Pedro Pascal talks hosting SNL on anniversary of his mother's passing and his sister Lux

Hollywood's latest sensation Pedro Pascal got candid in an intimate new interview with Esquire, opening up about his late mother, sister Lux, and finding fame later in life.

In the interview, Pascal recalled speaking with his late mother as he prepared to host Saturday Night Live on the anniversary of her passing.

Veronica Pascal tragically took her own life on February 4, 1999, and Pascal was scheduled to host SNL exactly 24 years later. 

To alleviate his own fears about 'bombing' on live television, Pascal began talking to her.

'I was so scared that week that I was talking to her,' he explained. Recalling returning home after rehearsals, he said, ' there would be that terror waiting for me—that practical fear of bombing in front of the world. And then I talked to her, and it was really comforting. I had sort of the realization that it would be nice to talk to her more.'

Pascal would tell his mother: 'I love you. I miss you. Thank you. I’m scared. I would love it if you would help me believe in myself, because I know you do. You know?'

Pascal was still struggling to make it big when his mother died, however was enjoying minor success in his profession.

At the time, he was 24 and had just moved from New York to Los Angeles.

After her passing, her rushed back to his native Chile to be with his family.

Pascal, who in total has three siblings, recalled feeling 'parental' towards his youngest siblings, sister Lux and brother Nicolás. At the time of her passing, Lux was just seven.

'They were very young kids, so much younger than me and my older sister, so even if they hadn’t lost a parent, we would still feel parental toward them. And I didn’t naively think I could fill a space like that, but I just always wanted to be like, I’m here,' he told the publication.

Afterwards, Pascal decided to honor his mother by taking on her last name as his stage name. 

The Mandalorian actor also gushed about his sister Lux, calling her one of the 'most powerful people and personalities' he knows.

Pascal refrained from speaking on Lux's behalf when asked how she had been holding up amid a turbulent time for transgender people. Lux came out as transgender in 2021.  

'But she is and has always been one of the most powerful people and personalities I’ve ever known. My protective side is lethal, but I need her more than she needs me,' he said.

Pascal also described the struggles he endured early in his career, admitting he was 'getting my ass f***ing kicked' as he pursued acting. 

Despite often getting passed up for roles he was close to landing, he received good feedback from casting directors which ultimately persuaded his representatives to continue working with him.  

'I guess that, and this delusional self-determination, and no real skill at anything else, is what kept me going,' he said.  

Early in his career, he thought it would all be 'over' if he didn't achieve major success by the time he was 29.

'I died so many deaths. My vision of it was that if I didn’t have some major exposure by the time I was twenty-nine years old, it was over, so I was constantly readjusting what it meant to commit my life to this profession, and giving up the idea of it looking like I thought it would when I was a kid. 

'There were so many good reasons to let that delusion go.' 

Now, Pascal is one of the biggest stars in Hollywood with starring roles in hit shows The Mandalorian and The Last Of Us.

The Last Of Us, which is based on the video game of the same name, stayed close to the plot of the original in season one. In the second part of the game, The Last Of Us Part II, Joel, the character Pascal plays, is killed off. 

Speaking of his fate in season two, should the show continue to follow the video game, he said: 'It wouldn’t make sense to follow the first game so faithfully only to stray severely from the path. So, yeah, that’s my honest answer.'

Pascal also described how he's learning to redefine what being middle-age means to him.  

'I had a moment of thinking, You’re in your forties and you don’t own a home? Grow up. But I’m relinquishing expectations around what it is to be middle-aged and what it means to be fully grown up. Why am I trying to force a square shape into a triangle?'

He sighs and adds, 'I just don’t want to make any decisions.'

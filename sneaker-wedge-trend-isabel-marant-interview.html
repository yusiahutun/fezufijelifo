Why I Cant Stop Wearing Sneaker Wedges

Two months ago, I was walking home from work when I saw a girl wearing a pair of Isabel Marant sneaker wedges. She had styled them with baggy dark-denim Bermuda shorts, a tight off-the-shoulder top wrapped around her collarbones like Saran Wrap, and layers of necklaces with chunky charms. The proportions were so perfect, it made my brain go analog: I forgot about the opportunity to take a sneaky photo and instead rushed home to sketch out the look in a notepad.

I couldn’t stop thinking about the shoe, so I ended up buying a pair. I wear them whenever I feel like my look needs something unexpected, mostly with shorts that would otherwise be too casual. The pillowy height makes me feel sexy in a cheeky way that isn’t too on the nose.

Edward Berthelot

A guest at Copenhagen Fashion Week last August wearing a pair of sneaker wedges outside of a show

Marant launched the original sneaker wedge, the Bekett trainer, almost exactly 11 years ago. Beyoncé famously wore them in her “Love on Top” music video, and every celebrity from Rihanna to Gisele Bündchen to Gwen Stefani owned a pair. So many editors wore them to Fashion Week in the 2010s that think pieces were published just to praise Marant for getting a heel-obsessed crowd to opt for something more comfortable. They were so popular, so beloved, so everywhere that you had to get on a six-month wait list to buy a pair—or score them marked up on eBay for over $1,000. And now, a decade later, they’re bubbling back up into the fashion zeitgeist.

Marant says she had the idea for the sneaker as a teen. “When I was younger, I was always wearing heels, but I was very tomboyish,” she says. “I always wanted to look taller, and I loved long legs. I used to hide some pieces of cork in my sneakers—to look taller and also to elongate my legs.”

The shoes launched at a moment when business casual had become the du jour club look for young adults, and they offered a chic alternative to blister-inducing pointy heels. Wearing a pair made the essential formula of blazer, bandeau, and chunky statement necklace appear less expected, less square. And customers appreciated the opportunity to look taller without sacrificing comfort. Marant says, “I remember you couldn’t go outside without seeing it. It was like everybody had that shoe.”

Alo Ceballos

Lindsay Lohan in a pair of sneaker wedges in 2012

I was in college then, and I used to lull myself to sleep every night scrolling through Tumblr, where the shoes were everywhere. Earlier last year, I noticed them popping up on TikTok—2023’s preferred app for bedtime perusal. Clara Perlmutter—or @tinyjewishgirl on TikTok—has garnered almost 900,000 followers for her getting-ready videos; she’s known for playful looks that often involve crinkled gold metallic Isabel Marant sneaker wedges.

Perlmutter has a long-standing relationship with the shoes. “My mom followed fashion and I saw the Isabel Marant ones over her shoulder on fashion blogs and I did NOT get them,” she tells me over email. Perlmutter decided to buy her own pair years later, wearing them exactly as Marant had intended them: “I am such a sneaker girl, but I’m short, and I bought them because I had the epiphany that I could wear them so my pants don’t drag on the ground. The Isabel Marant ones have such a massive tongue that my pants crinkle up above them in an attractive way. I normally wear them with low-rise wide-leg jeans or micro shorts. With micro shorts there’s a fab-leg lengthening effect.”

You can tell from the comments on her page how old (or young) her followers are. Some claim the sight of the shoes unlocks long-lost memories, while others, likely born after 2003, just want to know what they’re called. “I was drawn to sneaker wedges mainly because of their practical purpose, but also because they are divisive—which makes for good content,” Perlmutter admits.

Influencer and knitwear designer Lindsay Vrčkovnik, of the Instagram handle @linmick, also owns a pair of Isabel Marant sneaker wedges she wears frequently. On a post about the shoes last June, followers wrote things like, “The sneaker wedge. I’m fighting the urge.”

Both Perlmutter and Vrčkovnik style the sneaker in ways that are distinctively not 2012—no skinny jeans here. In a video from 2021, Perlmutter wore them with a gold tank layered with a cutout crochet knit, zebra-print jeans, and a vintage navy Calvin Klein trench passed down from her grandma. Vrčkovnik often wears hers with miniskirts and shorts, frequently layered with multiple pieces in a multitude of bold colors and prints

Marant, too, has sensed that we’re reentering a sneaker wedge moment. In 2021 she released the Balskee, a new version that is two inches taller than the original. “It’s really taking up new space in a different era and in a different way,” she says. “The world has evolved.”

Now that the fog of the 2010s has passed, those of us who get excited about getting dressed can see the shoe for what it really is: a freaky fun idea that feels right in line with what young people want. Sneaker wedges remind world-weary Gen Zers of a simpler era. They’re designed to be comfortable, which still matters to people used to wearing pandemic-era sneakers and slides. And they’re sexy, but not in an obvious way. Perlmutter sums it up: “They’re pretty universally fabulous if you’re daring enough.”

Tara Gonzalez is the Senior Fashion Editor at Harper’s Bazaar. Previously, she was the style writer at InStyle, founding commerce editor at Glamour, and fashion editor at Coveteur.

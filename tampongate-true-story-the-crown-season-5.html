Is Tampongate in The Crown Season 5 Based on a True Story?

Netflix's The Crown has never held back when it comes to the most scandalous aspects of royal life. Season 5 is no exception, with new episodes exploring the breakdown of Charles and Diana's marriage, as well as Charles' continued affair with Camilla Parker Bowles.

Although it's a little hard to imagine now, before they took the throne, King Charles III and Queen Consort Camilla found themselves embroiled in a very public scandal. Here's what you need to know about the very aptly-titled Tampongate, which is featured in the fifth episode of The Crown Season 5.

What is Tampongate?

Tampongate, which has also been dubbed Camillagate, refers to a private phone conversation between Charles and Camilla, which took place when they were still married to their respective spouses. The conversation, which happened in December 1989, per Vanity Fair, was secretly recorded and later leaked to the press. Due to the explicit nature of Charles and Camilla's discussion, the leaked recording caused an inordinate amount of drama for the royal family.

Importantly, in 1989, King Charles, then still a prince, was married to Princess Diana, while Camilla was married to Andrew Parker Bowles. Not only did the recording suggest that Camilla and Charles had been engaged in an extramarital relationship for many years, its graphic content also raised some eyebrows.

As revealed in the actual transcript (shared by TIME), Charles told Camilla he wished he could live in her "trousers," so as to be close to her at all times. She retorted that the prince could be reincarnated as "a pair of knickers," to which he joked that, knowing his "luck," he'd end up as a tampon instead. Needless to say, the world was shocked to learn of Charles' late night phone chats with his former girlfriend.

Who leaked Charles and Camilla's intimate conversation?

Per the Los Angeles Times, Tampongate "was said to have been recorded by an amateur radio buff who picked it up on high-tech scanning equipment in December, 1989." At the time, Camilla was allegedly at home, while Charles was reportedly staying at a friend's country house, the publication reported.

A transcript of Charles and Camilla’s private phone conversation is published in 1993.

A transcript of Charles and Camilla's conversation was initially published by an Australian magazine in January 1993, with British tabloids following suit later that month, per UPI. Of his decision to publish details of the phone call, editor of British tabloid the Sunday Mirror, Colin Myler, said (via UPI), "Yes, I will be criticized. Yes, I will be damned ... But why should the people of Britain be treated with such hypocrisy and contempt?"

It was also alleged that the royal family might have been under surveillance by a British Intelligence agency, such as MI-5, per the Los Angeles Times. This claim arose as additional recordings of the royal family, including Princess Diana's "Squidygate" conversation with James Gilbey, leaked around the same time. However, the surveillance claim has never been substantiated.

How did Tampongate affect the royal family?

Understandably, Charles' leaked conversation with his rumored mistress sent shockwaves through the monarchy. In January 1993, the Los Angeles Times suggested that the leaked phone call "raised doubts in political and editorial circles ... that [Charles] will ever be king." While Charles has since acceded the throne, the scandal has seemingly haunted him for decades.

Discussing the scandal, royal biographer Sally Bedell Smith told Vanity Fair, "It did him and her a lot of damage. His popularity plummeted and people were questioning his fitness to be king." Meanwhile, in his biography of the king, Howard Hodgson wrote, via the Express, "He remains deeply ashamed of the embarrassment that he caused his mother, deeply sorry for the pain it caused both his and Camilla’s children."

Keith Bernstein//Netflix

Although Tampongate isn't necessarily the reason for Charles and Camilla's respective divorces, the timing of the leak is certainly suspect. The contents of Tampongate hit the press in early 1993, soon after Charles and Diana announced their separation in December 1992; they would finalize their divorce in August 1996. Meanwhile, Camilla and Andrew announced their intention to divorce in January 1995.

What did Princess Diana think about Tampongate?

Despite having her own private conversation with a male friend leaked, Princess Diana apparently revelled in her ex's schadenfreude. In fact, in 1997, Diana's former personal protection officer, Ken Wharfe, wrote a very telling book called Guarding Diana: Protecting The Princess Around The World. According to Wharfe (via The Independent), Diana reacted to the leak of Charles and Camilla's phone call by saying, "Game, set and match."

Any initial victory Diana might have allegedly felt upon having her husband's affair confirmed apparently dissipated quite quickly. "Later, however, she told me that she had been genuinely shocked by some of the baser comments, particular the Prince's tampon reference," Wharfe claimed in his book. "'It's just sick,' she said repeatedly."

Is The Crown's recreation of Tampongate accurate?

Viewers might be shocked to find out that The Crown's portrayal of Tampongate is actually very accurate. In fact, the script sticks pretty closely to the real transcript of Charles and Camilla's X-rated phone call.

Discussing the show's re-creation of one of the royal family's most embarrassing moments, biographer Sally Bedell Smith told Vanity Fair, "It did strike me as more than ironic that when so much of the series is made up ... that it would seek refuge in the factual when it suited their purposes and demean[ed the royal family]." She continued, "So here we do have an instance in a series that is largely fictional of them recreating the facts of what [Charles and Camilla] said because the [actual words] put them in a very bad light."

Dominic West, who portrays Prince Charles in The Crown's fifth season, discussed the furore surrounding Tampongate, telling Entertainment Weekly, "I remember thinking it was something so sordid and deeply, deeply embarrassing [at the time]."

Keith Bernstein//Netflix

However, with the gift of hindsight, West views the leaking of Charles' personal conversation very differently. "Looking back on it, and having to play it, what you're conscious of is that the blame was not with these two people, two lovers, who were having a private conversation," he explained. "What's really [clear now] is how invasive and disgusting was the press's attention to it, that they printed it out verbatim and you could call a number and listen to the actual tape. I think it made me extremely sympathetic towards the two of them and what they'd gone through."

As for how closely The Crown's Tampongate episode aligns with fact, fans can peruse the full transcript, which was published by British tabloid the People on January 17, 1993, via the Mirror. There you can read an excerpt of the real thing, so you can decide for yourself just how accurate The Crown's fifth season is.
